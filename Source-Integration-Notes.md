### 2. Parenti (1997) - Blackshirts and Reds, Ch. 1: "Rational Fascism"

**Central Thesis:** Fascism is not irrational chaos but a rational political project to serve capitalist class interests. It uses irrational ideology (nationalism, racism, leader-worship) to achieve rational class goals (crushing labor, cutting wages, privatization, subsidies to big business).

**Key Quotable Passages:**

On the class function of fascism:
> "Fascism may have been a 'revolution against modernity' for the masses, but for the moneyed classes, it was no revolution at all, just profitable business."

> "Is fascism merely a dictatorial force in the service of capitalism? That may not be all it is, but that certainly is an important part of fascism's raison d'être."

> "Fascism is a false revolution. It cultivates the appearance of popular politics and a revolutionary aura without offering a genuine revolutionary class content. It propagates a 'New Order' while serving the same old moneyed interests."

On rational manipulation of irrational symbols:
> "Much of politics is the rational manipulation of irrational symbols. Certainly, this is true of fascist ideology, whose emotive appeals have served a class-control function."

**Excellent teaching excerpt - the play *Schlageter* (1933):**
A dialogue between father (old socialist) and son (young Nazi) about "Volk community" vs. class struggle:

> **Son:** The class struggle is dying out... we have the Volk community.
> **Father:** And that's a slogan?
> **Son:** No, it's an experience!
> **Father:** My God, our class struggle, our strikes, they weren't an experience, eh?... Tell me how do you actually envision it? Poor, rich, healthy, upper, lower, all this ceases with you, eh?
> **Son:** Look, Papa, upper, lower, poor, rich, that always exists. It is only the importance one places on that question that's decisive... we believe in human existence as a whole.

This excerpt perfectly illustrates how fascism dismisses class analysis through monistic appeals to "national unity."

**On economic policies:**
- Both Italy and Germany: crushed unions, abolished minimum wage, cut wages (25-50%), reintroduced child labor
- Privatized state enterprises, massive subsidies to heavy industry
- Raised taxes on workers, lowered/eliminated taxes on rich
- Result: industrial profits rose, poverty increased

**On contemporary relevance:**
> "In the USA, they propagate populist-sounding appeals to the 'ordinary Middle American' while quietly pressing for measures that serve the interests of the wealthiest individuals and corporations."

**Integration with unit:**
- **Day 2:** Parenti's analysis helps students see the CLASS function beneath the RHETORICAL surface of contemporary voices
- **Day 3:** Complements Eco—while Eco describes what fascism looks like culturally, Parenti explains what it DOES economically
- **Teacher background:** Understanding the class function prevents the common error of treating fascism as mere irrationality
- **The play excerpt** could be powerful for discussion—shows a real historical moment of a family divided by fascist appeal

**Potential handout addition:**
The Schlageter dialogue could work as a short primary source—students analyze how the son dismisses class concerns through appeals to national community.

### NEW: Toscano (2023) - Late Fascism: Race, Capitalism and the Politics of Crisis

**This is considered one of the most important recent analyses of fascism.**

**Central Concept:** "Late fascism" - like "late capitalism," fascism varies by socioeconomic context. Today's fascism is paradoxically **nostalgic for synchronicity** - for the Fordist heyday of industrial modernity, the "forgotten" white worker. It's out of time, fantasizing about a past that never quite existed.

**Key Theoretical Insights:**

**On fascism as process, not event:**
> "Rather than treating fascism as a singular event... we need 'to see fascism within the totality of its process.'"

**Du Bois's framing - the unifying thread:**
> "W. E. B. Du Bois gave this core a name, still usable today: 'the counter-revolution of property'. For all their deep differences... the Ku Klux Klan terrorism against Black Reconstruction, the rise of squadrismo against labour organising in Italy, or the murderous codification of neoliberalism in Chile's constitution can all be understood under that heading."

**On "phony fanaticism" (Adorno via Toscano) - CRUCIAL CONCEPT:**
> "Just as little as people believe in the depth of their hearts that the Jews are the devil, do they completely believe in the leader. They do not really identify themselves with him but act this identification, perform their own enthusiasm, and thus participate in their leader's performance. It is through this performance that they strike a balance between their continuously mobilized instinctual urges and the historical stage of enlightenment they have reached, which cannot be revoked arbitrarily. It is probably the suspicion of this fictitiousness of their own 'group psychology' which makes fascist crowds so merciless and unapproachable. If they would stop for a second, the whole performance would go to pieces, and they would be left to panic."

This is crucial: contemporary fascism may be **performed** rather than deeply believed. The conviction is hollow, which makes crowds "merciless and unapproachable" - if they stopped, the whole performance would collapse.

**"Psychoanalysis in reverse":**
> "The assemblage of more or less constantly manipulated devices to keep people in permanent psychic bondage, to increase and reinforce neurotic and even psychotic behavior culminating in perpetual dependency on a 'leader' or on institutions or products."

Fascism doesn't free people from repression - it keeps them in "permanent psychic bondage."

**On "manipulated seriality" (Sartre via Toscano):**
> "Manipulated seriality is the heart of fascist politics... it is not just any mass that fascism conjures up, but an other-directed mass that never 'fuses' into a group, a mass which must produce macro-effects at the bidding of the group 'other-directing' it, all the while remaining dispersed."

Fascism works on atomized individuals, turning them into false totalities (nation, race) while keeping them dispersed. The "white working class" is a manipulated seriality, not a real class subject.

**On whiteness as supplement:**
> "Whiteness is the supplement to a politically void or spectral notion of the working class; it is what allows a pseudo-collective agency to be imbued with a (toxic) psychosocial content."

**Bloch on "non-contemporaneity":**
Fascism exploits people who are "out of sync" with capitalist modernity - peasants, petty bourgeoisie, those whose traditional lifeways have been destroyed.

> "Fascism is a 'swindle of fulfilment' - it exploits authentic desires fraudulently."

**Contemporary twist - nostalgia for Fordism:**
> "The fascistic tendencies finding expression in the 2016 US election... are seemingly driven by a nostalgia for synchronicity, for the 'Fordist' heyday of Big Capital and Big Labour (generally coded as male and white)... the industrial worker-citizen, the authorised emblem of a post-utopian depoliticised post-war modernity, now reappears... in the guise of the 'forgotten men', the 'non-synchronous people' of the political present."

**On fascism's "unity trick":**
> "Rather than an emancipatory concern with equality, fascism promotes a 'repressive egalitarianism', based on an identity of subjection and a fraternity of hatred: 'The undercurrent of malicious egalitarianism, of the brotherhood of all-encompassing humiliation, is a component of fascist propaganda and fascism itself.'"

**Integration with unit:**
- **Teacher background (essential):** Toscano's analysis helps explain WHY contemporary fascist rhetoric sounds nostalgic ("Make X Great Again") and WHY it's performative
- **Day 2:** The "phony fanaticism" concept could inform discussion of contemporary voices - are these figures genuine believers or performers?
- **Day 3:** Complements Eco by explaining the MECHANISM of fascist appeal (manipulated seriality, unfulfilled desires channeled into false totalities)
- **Advanced students:** The concept of "non-contemporaneity" - why people feel "left behind" by modernity
- **The "unity trick"** connects to Eco's "selective populism" - everyone is equal in their subjection to the leader

**Too dense for student handouts** but essential for teacher understanding of the contemporary landscape.

### 3. Sontag (1975) - Fascinating Fascism

**Central Thesis:** Fascism has a distinct aesthetic that persists beyond its political defeat. Analyzing Leni Riefenstahl's work and the eroticization of SS imagery, Sontag demonstrates that fascist aesthetics have detached from explicit politics and circulate freely in consumer culture.

**THE KEY DEFINITION (p.91) - Essential for Day 4:**
> "Fascist aesthetics... flow from (and justify) a preoccupation with situations of control, submissive behavior, extravagant effort, and the endurance of pain; they endorse two seemingly opposite states, egomania and servitude. The relations of domination and enslavement take the form of a characteristic pageantry: the massing of groups of people; the turning of people into things; the multiplication or replication of things; and the grouping of people/things around an all-powerful, hypnotic leader-figure or force. The fascist dramaturgy centers on the orgiastic transactions between mighty forces and their puppets, uniformly garbed and shown in ever swelling numbers. Its choreography alternates between ceaseless motion and a congealed, static, 'virile' posing. Fascist art glorifies surrender, it exalts mindlessness, it glamorizes death."

This single passage provides the complete theoretical framework for understanding Mishima.

**Riefenstahl's confession (p.85) - What "beauty" means in fascist aesthetics:**
> "What fascinated me about the Nuba was the image of a proud, beautiful, and wild people... their sense of beauty and ritual and art. I wanted to make them known to the world."

Sontag comments: The beautiful "wild" people of Africa parallel the beautiful "wild" Germans of Triumph of the Will - both represent the aestheticization of a supposedly pre-modern purity.

**On the body as container (p.91-92):**
> "The fascist ideal is to transform sexual energy into a 'spiritual' force, for the benefit of the community. The erotic (that is, women) is always present as a temptation, with the most admirable response being a heroic repression of the sexual impulse."

**SONTAG EXPLICITLY MENTIONS MISHIMA (p.100):**
> "The eroticism of Nazism is more and more visible. In another context, it emerges from the preoccupations with physical perfection and death that animate such overwrought works as... Mishima's Confessions of a Mask and Sun and Steel."

This is powerful: Sontag herself connects the aesthetic pattern she's analyzing directly to the Mishima texts we're teaching. She sees Mishima as exemplifying the same fascist aesthetic she finds in Riefenstahl.

**On the persistence of fascist aesthetics (p.97):**
> "Art which evokes the themes of fascist aesthetics is popular now, and for most people it is probably no more than a variant of camp... But fascist aesthetics has a distinctive place among the fictions which express the desire to die."

**Key insight - fascist aesthetics as death-worship:**
> "What is most beautiful in virile men is something feminine; what is most beautiful in feminine women is something masculine... The beautiful in fascism is death; death is the moment of highest vitality."

**The SS uniform analysis (p.99-100):**
Sontag analyzes how SS regalia has become eroticized in contemporary culture - "the color is black, the material is leather, the seduction is beauty, the justification is honesty, the aim is ecstasy, the fantasy is death."

**Integration with unit:**

**For Day 4 (Mishima) - ESSENTIAL:**
- The p.91 definition passage could be read aloud before students engage with Mishima
- Sontag's explicit mention of Mishima validates the connection we're making
- The "containment of vital forces" / "endurance of pain" framework maps directly onto Sun and Steel

**For teacher background:**
- Sontag explains WHY fascist aesthetics persist: they tap into deep currents of sexuality and death
- The "two seemingly opposite states, egomania and servitude" resolves the paradox of fascism: total submission to the leader enables fantasies of total power

**Potential handout excerpt:**
The p.91 definition passage is dense but readable for grade 10. Could give students this passage before the Mishima materials and ask: "What elements of this description do you recognize in Mishima's writing?"

**Connection to Benjamin:**
Where Benjamin provides the political analysis (aestheticization of politics), Sontag provides the psychological/erotic dimension. Together they explain both WHY fascism aestheticizes and HOW that aestheticization feels.

### 3. Benjamin (1935) - The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction

**Central concept:** The "aestheticization of politics"

**Full key passage (Epilogue, p.19):**
> "Fascism attempts to organize the newly created proletarian masses without affecting the property structure which the masses strive to eliminate. Fascism sees its salvation in giving these masses not their right, but instead a chance to express themselves. The masses have a right to change property relations; Fascism seeks to give them an expression while preserving property. The logical result of Fascism is the introduction of aesthetics into political life."

> "All efforts to render politics aesthetic culminate in one thing: war. War and war only can set a goal for mass movements on the largest scale while respecting the traditional property system."

**The formula:** Communism responds by politicizing art; Fascism responds by aestheticizing politics.

**CRITICAL PRIMARY SOURCE - Marinetti's manifesto on the Ethiopian war (quoted by Benjamin, p.19-20):**
> "War is beautiful because it establishes man's dominion over the subjugated machinery by means of gas masks, terrifying megaphones, flame throwers, and small tanks. War is beautiful because it initiates the dreamt-of metallization of the human body. War is beautiful because it enriches a flowering meadow with the fiery orchids of machine guns. War is beautiful because it combines the gunfire, the cannonades, the cease-fire, the scents, and the stench of putrefaction into a symphony."

This Marinetti passage is PERFECT for teaching fascist aesthetics - makes the aesthetic dimension viscerally clear.

**Benjamin's devastating conclusion (p.20):**
> "Mankind, which in Homer's time was an object of contemplation for the Olympian gods, now is one for itself. Its self-alienation has reached such a degree that it can experience its own destruction as an aesthetic pleasure of the first order. This is the situation of politics which Fascism is rendering aesthetic."

**Relevance to unit:**
- Provides theoretical grounding for why fascism is fundamentally aesthetic
- Explains why spectacle, ritual, and beauty are not incidental but essential to fascism
- Connects to Mishima: he made politics into art, life into performance, death into aesthetic statement
- The Marinetti quote shows fascism's beauty-of-destruction logic explicitly

**Suggested integration:**
- **The Marinetti quote could be a primary source for Day 4** - students analyze how fascism aestheticizes violence
- Teacher background for Day 4 facilitation
- The "aestheticization of politics" phrase is essential vocabulary

### 4. Paxton (2004) - The Anatomy of Fascism

**This is a scholarly overview that complements Eco's framework.**

**Paxton's Definition (p.22):**
> "Fascism may be defined as a form of political behavior marked by obsessive preoccupation with community decline, humiliation, or victimhood and by compensatory cults of unity, energy, and purity, in which a mass-based party of committed nationalist militants, working in uneasy but effective collaboration with traditional elites, abandons democratic liberties and pursues with redemptive violence and without ethical or legal restraints goals of internal cleansing and external expansion."

**Paxton's "Mobilizing Passions" (p.23):**
1. A sense of overwhelming crisis beyond the reach of any traditional solutions
2. The primacy of the group, toward which one has duties superior to every right, whether individual or universal, and the subordination of the individual to it
3. The belief that one's group is a victim, a sentiment that justifies any action, without legal or moral limits, against its enemies, both internal and external
4. Dread of the group's decline under the corrosive effects of individualistic liberalism, class conflict, and alien influences
5. The need for closer integration of a purer community, by consent if possible, or by exclusionary violence if necessary
6. The need for authority by natural chiefs (always male), culminating in a national chieftain who alone is capable of incarnating the group's historical destiny
7. The superiority of the leader's instincts over abstract and universal reason
8. The beauty of violence and the efficacy of will, when they are devoted to the group's success
9. The right of the chosen people to dominate others without restraint from any kind of human or divine law, right being decided by the sole criterion of the group's prowess within a Darwinian struggle

**Eco vs. Paxton:**
- Eco's 14 features are more diagnostic (what does fascism look like?)
- Paxton's 9 passions are more psychological (what does fascism feel like?)
- Both describe fascism as syndrome/pattern rather than fixed ideology
- Could use Paxton to supplement Eco or as teacher background

**Key distinction - Fascism vs. Authoritarianism (pp.19-21):**
- Authoritarians prefer passive populations and limited state
- Fascists want to engage and excite the public
- Authoritarians rely on traditional elites (Church, army, landowners)
- Fascists create parallel institutions and mass mobilization
- Franco's Spain = authoritarian; Mussolini's Italy = fascist

**Fascism as "phenomenon of failed democracies" (p.19):**
> "Fascism, for Salvemini, meant setting aside democracy and due process in public life, to the acclamation of the street. It is a phenomenon of failed democracies."

**Suggested integration:**
- Paxton's definition could appear in teacher notes
- "Mobilizing passions" could be a second framework for advanced students
- Fascism/authoritarianism distinction useful for teacher when students ask about Orbán/Meloni

### 5. Mishima (1968) - Sun and Steel

**This is the core text for understanding Mishima's aesthetic project. Dense, philosophical, highly quotable.**

**Central Thesis:** The antinomy of words and flesh. Mishima spent his early life trapped in language; bodybuilding became his attempt to escape the corrosive effects of intellectualism and achieve direct, wordless existence through the body.

**Key Passages for Handout Expansion:**

**On the body/words antinomy:**
> "In my case, words came first of all; then—belatedly, with every appearance of extreme reluctance, and already clothed in concepts—came the flesh. It was already, as goes without saying, sadly wasted by words."

> "The muscles that I thus created were at one and the same time simple existence and works of art; they even, paradoxically, possessed a certain abstract nature."

**On muscles and the paradox of creation:**
> "Their one fatal flaw was that they were too closely involved with the life process, which decreed that they should decline and perish with the decline of life itself."

**On body requiring death:**
> "Specifically, I cherished a romantic impulse towards death, yet at the same time I required a strictly classical body as its vehicle... A powerful, tragic frame and sculpturesque muscles were indispensable in a romantically noble death."

> "The thing that ultimately saves the flesh from being ridiculous is the element of death that resides in the healthy, vigorous body; it is this, I realized, that sustains the dignity of the flesh."

**On pain as proof of existence:**
> "Pain, I came to feel, might well prove to be the sole proof of the persistence of consciousness within the flesh, the sole physical expression of consciousness."

**On the group (CRUCIAL for fascist dimension):**
> "Only through the group, I realized—through sharing the suffering of the group—could the body reach that height of existence that the individual alone could never attain... The group must be open to death—which meant, of course, that it must be a community of warriors."

> "In this way, the group for me had come to represent a bridge, a bridge that, once crossed, left no means of return."

**On sun and steel as transformative agents:**
> "The sun and the steel were simultaneously corroding me and building me up, strengthening my muscles while tirelessly pouring a kind of death into me."

**On action vs. thought:**
> "I had never yet managed to achieve anything resembling direct action. To tell the truth, even the acquisition of my muscles had been an act of thought."

**On the ultimate fusion:**
> "The moment when body and spirit coincide is the only true existence... a fusion of death and life, of corruption and incorruption."

**Connections to Eco's Features:**
- **Action for action's sake:** The entire project is about escaping thought into deed
- **Cult of heroic death:** Death is explicitly the culmination of physical perfection
- **Machismo:** The body as masculine ideal, contempt for his former "feminine" self
- **Life is permanent warfare:** The "community of warriors" concept
- **Rejection of modernism:** Postwar Japan as decadent, corrupted by Western intellectualism

**Connections to Sontag:**
- "Containment of vital forces" - Mishima's muscles as contained explosive energy
- "Bodies as perfect specimens" - the sculpturesque ideal
- "The perfect body exists to be destroyed gloriously" - exact match
- "Fascist aesthetics flow from situations of control, submissive behavior, extravagant effort, and the endurance of pain" - Mishima's training regimen

- The body/words antinomy (central thesis)
- The group/warriors concept (connects to fascist collectivism)
- Pain as proof of existence
- The fatal flaw of muscles (decline with life)

1. Opening passage on words/flesh antinomy

### 6. Mishima (1961) - Patriotism (Yūkoku)

**Central Situation:** Set during the February 26, 1936 incident—a failed coup by young army officers attempting to restore direct imperial rule. Lieutenant Shinji Takeyama learns his closest friends have become mutineers. Faced with the impossible choice of fighting his friends or betraying the Emperor, he and his wife Reiko choose ritual suicide.

**Why This Story Matters for the Unit:**
Mishima filmed himself playing the lieutenant in 1966. In 1970, he enacted this story in reality—staging a failed coup at a military base and committing seppuku. His life became his art; his death was the completion of his aesthetic project.

**Key Themes (from scholarly analysis):**

**Death as Aesthetic Creation:**
The story transforms suicide from tragic inevitability into deliberate artistic act. Mishima maintains "grace and control" while depicting graphic horror. The narrative suggests that beauty and violence are inseparable—one cannot exist without the other.

**Eros and Thanatos Merged:**
The couple's final lovemaking occurs *because* death approaches. Passion becomes "all the more ecstatic due to death's proximity." Sexual climax and death become parallel consummations. Key insight: *intensity of feeling requires proximity to annihilation.*

**The Grotesque and Sublime Simultaneously:**
Mishima depicts "scatological horror" without breaking the elevated tone. Entrails slip out while gold markings glint. This maintains aesthetic dignity amid bodily degradation—the key paradox of fascist aesthetics.

**Individuality Subsumed:**
Reiko exemplifies how fascist aesthetics dissolve the self into larger structures. "Ever since her marriage her husband's existence had been her own existence." This is not pathology but the logical endpoint of self-surrender to a greater cause.

**"Death...the Patria":**
Crucially, one scholar argues the story's title is misleading. The couple's ultimate loyalty may not be to nation or Emperor but to *death itself*. Death becomes the fatherland—the thing to which all sacrifice is owed.

**Key Quotable Passages:**

On beauty and death merged:
> "Looking at the slender white figure of his wife the lieutenant experienced a bizarre excitement... To have every moment of death observed by those beautiful eyes — it was like being borne to death on a gentle, fragrant breeze."

On masculine beauty at its apex:
> "The lieutenant always looked well in uniform, but now, as he contemplated death with severe brows and firmly closed lips, he revealed what was perhaps masculine beauty at its most superb."

On what the sacrifice is FOR (the fascist objects of devotion):
> "In the radiant, bride-like figure of his white-robed wife the lieutenant seemed to see a vision of all those things he had loved and for which he was to lay down his life — The Imperial Household, The Nation, The Army Flag."

On death as public performance:
> "What he was about to perform was an act in his public capacity as a soldier... It was his conduct on the battlefield that he was now to display."

Mishima's summary of his aesthetic philosophy:
> "Perfect purity is possible if you turn your life into a line of poetry written with a splash of blood."

Additional descriptions from scholarly summaries:
- Reiko's body: "The whiteness and richness of the stomach and hips was like milk brimming in a great bowl"
- The death face: "half departed from the lieutenant's personal possession and had become the bust above a dead soldier's memorial"

**Integration with Unit:**

**For Day 4 (Mishima):**
- Patriotism is the clearest illustration of death-as-aesthetic-project
- The 1966 film + 1970 reality connection shows Mishima wasn't theorizing—he meant it
- The eros/thanatos fusion directly demonstrates Sontag's analysis
- Students can see: this is what it looks like when someone fully commits to fascist aesthetics

**For the "Seduction Question":**
Patriotism reveals what fascism offers that liberal democracy cannot:
- **Intensity:** Normal life feels pale; death makes everything vivid
- **Shared sacrifice:** Ultimate intimacy through dying together
- **Transcendence:** Individual dissolution into something larger
- **Meaning:** Every moment matters because it's the last
- **Beauty:** Even horror becomes sublime through aesthetic framing

**Connection to Sontag (p.91):**
Patriotism perfectly exemplifies: "Fascist aesthetics...flow from situations of control, submissive behavior, extravagant effort, and the endurance of pain; they endorse two seemingly opposite states, egomania and servitude."

### 7. Mishima (1949) - Confessions of a Mask (Kamen no Kokuhaku)

**Central Situation:** Autobiographical novel tracing the narrator Kochan's childhood and adolescence in Imperial Japan. He discovers he is homosexual in a society that violently suppresses such desires, and develops a "mask" of normalcy to survive.

**Why This Matters for the Unit:**
This is the ur-text for understanding Mishima. Written at 24, it established themes he would pursue until his death: the mask as survival strategy, the eroticization of violence and death, the connection between repressed desire and militarism.

**Key Themes (from scholarly analysis):**

**The Mask as Social Performance:**
Kochan develops a false personality to present to the world. He believes "everybody around him is also hiding their true feelings from each other, everybody participating in a 'reluctant masquerade.'" The mask is not what lies behind it but its very existence—Mishima shows a man who has "tried, unsuccessfully, to turn himself into the mask that he is only supposed to wear."

**Connection to Benjamin's "aestheticization of politics":** The mask theme shows how fascist cultures demand performance of identity. Everyone acts conviction they may not feel.

**The St. Sebastian Scene (Famous Passage):**
Kochan's sexual awakening occurs viewing Guido Reni's painting of St. Sebastian's martyrdom—a beautiful young man pierced by arrows.

**Key Quotable Passages:**

On the painting:
> "A remarkably handsome youth was bound naked to the trunk of the tree. His crossed hands were raised high..."

> "His white and matchless nudity gleams against a background of dusk. His muscular arms...are raised at a graceful angle..."

On his physical reaction:
> "That day, the instant I looked upon the picture, my entire being trembled with some pagan joy. My blood soared up..."

On beauty-in-suffering:
> "Two lone arrows cast their tranquil and graceful shadows upon the smoothness of his skin, like the shadows of a bough..."

> "It is not pain that hovers about his straining chest...but some flicker of melancholy pleasure like music."

On transformation/identification:
> "I want to change into him," thinking, "I want to be him."

Mishima describes this as "the beginning of my 'bad habit'"—linking aesthetic response to the beautiful suffering body with sexual awakening. The real can only be apprehended through the aesthetic.

**Homosexuality and Militarism:**
The novel reveals how Imperial Japan's martial culture both suppressed and sublimated homoerotic desire. The mask Kochan wears "conceals his desire for men—and the sadistic scenarios of destroying or consuming the male body that excite his lust—in a martial culture that denies all such longings even as it arguably sublimates them as militarism and imperialism."

**Key insight:** Fascist aesthetics channel forbidden desires into sanctioned violence. The same impulses that would be punished as homosexuality become celebrated as warrior spirit.

**Spirit vs. Flesh:**
Kochan idealizes women spiritually while experiencing "bloody, excremental lust" only for men. This creates a fundamental split between spirit and flesh that prevents normal life. The only resolution is through aestheticized violence.

**Integration with Unit:**

**For Day 4 (Mishima):**
- The St. Sebastian passage could be excerpted (visual + text)
- Shows origins of Mishima's death-beauty fusion
- Explains WHY the body becomes so important in Sun and Steel—it's compensation for early physical weakness and channeling of forbidden desire

**For Teacher Background:**
- Helps explain Mishima's trajectory: repression → bodybuilding → private army → coup → seppuku
- The mask theme connects to Toscano's "phony fanaticism"—performed conviction

**Connection to Contemporary Fascism:**
One scholar notes Mishima's online fanbase consists of "radical right-wing youth, mourning what they regard as their dispossessed manhood." The novel's themes—masculinity crisis, desire channeled into violence, the mask of performed identity—remain relevant.

### 8. Clara Zetkin (1923) - Report on Fascism

**Context:** One of the earliest Marxist analyses of fascism, delivered to the Communist International just months after Mussolini's March on Rome. Zetkin was a German communist leader.

**Central Thesis:** Fascism is not mere reaction or "White Terror"—it has a genuine mass base among people who have been disappointed by BOTH capitalism AND the failure of socialist alternatives.

**Key Quotable Passages:**

On who joins fascism:
> "Fascism has become a sort of refuge for the politically shelterless."

> "These masses of disappointed socialist sympathizers are joined by large circles of the proletariat, of workers who have given up their faith not only in socialism, but also in their own class."

On fascism's two distinguishing features:
> "Fascism has two distinguishing features in all countries, namely the pretence of a revolutionary programme, which is cleverly adapted to the interests and demands of the large masses, and on the other hand, the application of the most brutal violence."

On the need to understand fascism's appeal:
> "We must realise that Fascism is a movement of the disappointed and of those whose existence is ruined... We must struggle ideologically, for the possession of the soul of these masses. We must realise that they are not only trying to escape from their present tribulations, but that they are longing for a new philosophy."

On fascism's revolutionary disguise:
> "The cause of the first success of the Fascisti was that it made its start with a revolutionary gesture."

**Who fascism recruits (Zetkin's list):**
- Impoverished petty bourgeoisie (small business owners ruined by economic crisis)
- Petty peasants (small farmers)
- Intellectuals (educated but unemployed)
- Ex-officers and unemployed soldiers
- Workers who have lost faith in their own class

**Key insight for the unit:**
Zetkin emphasizes that fascism PRETENDS to be revolutionary—it starts by attacking the existing order, claiming to fight for the little guy against "all exploiters." Mussolini initially demanded the execution of capitalists and the overthrow of the monarchy. Only after gaining power did fascism reveal its true function: serving capital.

**Connection to "Why Fascism is Seductive":**
Zetkin's analysis adds the CLASS dimension: fascism appeals to people who wanted change but were failed by both the status quo AND by the left. They are "longing for a new philosophy"—fascism offers one.

**For teacher background:**
- Complements Parenti's "rational fascism" thesis
- Explains WHY fascism sounds revolutionary (because it has to, to recruit)
- The "politically shelterless" concept is useful for discussing contemporary movements

### 9. Leon Trotsky (1930s, compiled 1944) - Fascism: What It Is and How to Fight It

**Context:** A compilation of Trotsky's writings from 1930-1940, primarily focused on the rise of fascism in Italy and Germany. Written in real-time as fascism was ascending, not as a postmortem.

**Central Thesis:** Fascism is a mass movement of the "crazed petty bourgeoisie" that serves finance capital's interests. It arises specifically when the working class shows "complete incapacity to take into its own hands the fate of society" - i.e., when revolutionary opportunities are squandered.

**Key Quotable Passages:**

On fascism as a plebian mass movement:
> "The fascist movement in Italy was a spontaneous movement of large masses, with new leaders from the rank and file. It is a plebian movement in origin, directed and financed by big capitalist powers. It issued forth from the petty bourgeoisie, the slum proletariat, and even to a certain extent from the proletarian masses."

On what makes fascism distinct from ordinary dictatorship:
> "When a state turns fascist, it does not mean only that the forms and methods of government are changed... but it means first of all for the most part that the workers' organizations are annihilated; that the proletariat is reduced to an amorphous state; and that a system of administration is created which penetrates deeply into the masses."

On the petty bourgeoisie as fascism's battering ram:
> "Through the fascist agency, capitalism sets in motion the masses of the crazed petty bourgeoisie and the bands of declassed and demoralized lumpenproletariat – all the countless human beings whom finance capital itself has brought to desperation and frenzy."

**THE KEY FORMULA - Hope vs. Despair:**
> "If the Communist Party is the party of revolutionary hope, then fascism, as a mass movement, is the party of counter-revolutionary despair."

This is crucial: fascism recruits from despair. When people lose hope that positive change is possible, they become available for fascism.

On fascism as "caricature of Jacobinism":
> "In the period of the decline of bourgeois society, the bourgeoisie again needs the 'plebian' method of resolving its no longer progressive but entirely reactionary tasks. In this sense, fascism is a caricature of Jacobinism."

On what fascism's ideology consists of:
> "In Germany, this historical function is fulfilled by National Socialism (Nazism), a broad current whose ideology is composed of all the putrid vapors of disintegrating bourgeois society."

On fascism's historic function:
> "The historic function of fascism is to smash the working class, destroy its organizations, and stifle political liberties when the capitalists find themselves unable to govern and dominate with the help of democratic machinery."

On fascism as the petty bourgeoisie's self-destruction:
> "It may be said that fascism is the act of placing the petty bourgeoisie at the disposal of its most bitter enemies. In this way, big capital ruins the middle classes and then, with the help of hired fascist demagogues, incites the despairing petty bourgeoisie against the worker."

On why the petty bourgeoisie needs a "leader":
> "The petty bourgeoisie is economically dependent and politically atomized. That is why it cannot conduct an independent policy. It needs a 'leader' who inspires it with confidence."

On how fascism gives the illusion of agency:
> "Fascism unites and arms the scattered masses. Out of human dust, it organizes combat detachments. It thus gives the petty bourgeoisie the illusion of being an independent force. It begins to imagine that it will really command the state."

**Trotsky's "historical law":**
> "We may set it down as a historical law: fascism was able to conquer only in those countries where the conservative labor parties prevented the proletariat from utilizing the revolutionary situation and seizing power."

**THE COMPLETE CYCLE (essential summary):**
> "Fascism is each time the final link of a specific political cycle composed of the following: the gravest crisis of capitalist society; the growth of the radicalization of the working class; the growth of sympathy toward the working class, and a yearning for change on the part of the rural and urban petty bourgeoisie; the extreme confusion of the big bourgeoisie; its cowardly and treacherous maneuvers aimed at avoiding the revolutionary climax; the exhaustion of the proletariat; growing confusion and indifference; the aggravation of the social crisis; the despair of the petty bourgeoisie, its yearning for change; the collective neurosis of the petty bourgeoisie, its readiness to believe in miracles, its readiness for violent measures; the growth of hostility towards the proletariat, which has deceived its expectations. These are the premises for a swift formation of a fascist party and its victory."

**On preconditions for fascism:**
> "For the latter [fascist dictatorship], there is first necessary a feeling of desperation of large masses of the people. When the revolutionary parties betray them, when the vanguard of workers shows its incapacity to lead the people to victory – then the farmers, the small business men, the unemployed, the soldiers, etc., become capable of supporting a fascist movement, but only then."

**Integration with Unit:**

**For Teacher Background:**
- Trotsky's "hope vs. despair" formula complements Zetkin's "politically shelterless" concept
- The "collective neurosis" and "readiness to believe in miracles" connects to Toscano's "phony fanaticism"
- The "out of human dust" passage connects to Toscano's "manipulated seriality" - atomized individuals formed into false totalities

**For the Seduction Question:**
Trotsky adds the class dimension: fascism offers the atomized, ruined petty bourgeoisie "the illusion of being an independent force." It makes the powerless feel powerful—even as it actually places them at the disposal of their enemies.

**Contemporary relevance:**
The cycle Trotsky describes—crisis, radicalization, failed alternatives, despair, fascism—is recognizable today. When mainstream politics fails to address real grievances, people become available for authoritarian solutions.

### 10. Georgi Dimitrov (1935) - The Fascist Offensive and the Tasks of the Communist International

**Context:** Dimitrov's report to the Seventh World Congress of the Communist International, delivered August 2, 1935. This is one of the most influential Marxist analyses of fascism, delivered just two years after Hitler's seizure of power and Dimitrov's own dramatic confrontation with Göring at the Reichstag Fire Trial.

**THE FAMOUS "DIMITROV THESIS" - Definition of Fascism:**
> "Fascism in power was correctly described by the Thirteenth Plenum of the Executive Committee of the Communist International as *the open terrorist dictatorship of the most reactionary, most chauvinistic and most imperialist elements of finance capital*."

This definition emphasizes fascism's CLASS CHARACTER - it serves finance capital, not the masses it claims to represent.

**On German fascism specifically:**
> "The most reactionary variety of fascism is the German type of fascism. It has the effrontery to call itself National Socialism, though it has nothing in common with socialism. German fascism is not only bourgeois nationalism, it is fiendish chauvinism. It is a government system of political gangsterism, a system of provocation and torture practised upon the working class... It is medieval barbarity and bestiality."

**KEY INSIGHT: How Fascism Recruits (THE SEDUCTION QUESTION):**

> "What is the source of the influence of fascism over the masses? Fascism is able to attract the masses because it demagogically appeals to their most urgent needs and demands. Fascism not only inflames prejudices that are deeply ingrained in the masses, but also plays on the better sentiments of the masses, on their sense of justice and sometimes even on their revolutionary traditions."

This is crucial: fascism appeals not just to prejudice but to **legitimate grievances and even noble sentiments**.

**On fascism's revolutionary disguise:**
> "Why do the German fascists, those lackeys of the bourgeoisie and mortal enemies of socialism, represent themselves to the masses as 'Socialists,' and depict their accession to power as a 'revolution'? Because they try to exploit the faith in revolution and the urge towards socialism that lives in the hearts of the mass of working people in Germany."

**On anti-capitalist demagogy:**
> "Fascism aims at the most unbridled exploitation of the masses but it approaches them with the most artful anti-capitalist demagogy, taking advantage of the deep hatred of the working people against the plundering bourgeoisie, the banks, trusts and financial magnates, and advancing those slogans which at the given moment are most alluring to the politically immature masses."

Examples of fascist slogans:
- Germany: "The general welfare is higher than the welfare of the individual"
- Italy: "Our state is not a capitalist, but a corporate state"
- Japan: "For Japan without exploitation"
- USA: "Share the wealth"

**On fascism's promise of "honest government":**
> "Fascism delivers up the people to be devoured by the most corrupt and venal elements, but comes before them with the demand for 'an honest and incorruptible government.' Speculating on the profound disillusionment of the masses in bourgeois-democratic governments, fascism hypocritically denounces corruption."

**On why youth are susceptible (CRUCIAL for Day 1 Socratic Circle):**
> "Fascism grasped the very acute need of the youth for militant activity, and enticed a considerable section of the youth into its fighting detachments. The new generation of young men and women has not experienced the horrors of war. They have felt the full weight of the economic crisis, unemployment and the disintegration of bourgeois democracy. But, seeing no prospects for the future, large sections of the youth proved to be particularly receptive to fascist demagogy, which depicted for them an alluring future should fascism succeed."

**On the conditions that allow fascism:**
> "Fascism was able to come to power primarily because the working class, owing to the policy of class collaboration with the bourgeoisie pursued by the Social-Democratic leaders, proved to be split, politically and organizationally disarmed, in face of the onslaught of the bourgeoisie."

**On fascism as a different STATE FORM (not just a different government):**
> "The accession to power of fascism is not an ordinary succession of one bourgeois government by another, but a substitution of one state form of class domination of the bourgeoisie -- bourgeois democracy -- by another form -- open terrorist dictatorship."

**On fascism's instability:**
> "The fascist dictatorship of the bourgeoisie is a ferocious power, but an unstable one."

Why? Because the "contrast between the anti-capitalist demagogy of fascism and its policy of enriching the monopolist bourgeoisie" eventually exposes fascism's true nature.

**On the ideological battle for national history:**
> "The fascists are rummaging through the entire history of every nation so as to be able to pose as the heirs and continuators of all that was exalted and heroic in its past, while all that was degrading or offensive to the national sentiments of the people they make use of as weapons against the enemies of fascism."

> "Communists who suppose that all this has nothing to do with the cause of the working class, who do nothing to enlighten the masses on the past of their people in a historically correct fashion... voluntarily hand over to the fascist falsifiers all that is valuable in the historical past of the nation, so that the fascists may fool the masses."

**Integration with Unit:**

**For Teacher Background:**
- Dimitrov provides the "official" Marxist definition of fascism that complements Eco's cultural features
- His analysis of HOW fascism recruits is essential for understanding the "seduction question"
- The anti-capitalist demagogy analysis helps explain why fascism sounds revolutionary

**For Day 1 (Socratic Circle):**
- Dimitrov's analysis of youth susceptibility directly supports the discussion questions about overwhelm, uncertainty, and wanting someone to take control
- His point about fascism appealing to "better sentiments" and "sense of justice" is key

**For Day 2 (Contemporary Voices):**
- The slogans Dimitrov quotes ("general welfare higher than individual," "honest government," etc.) directly parallel contemporary populist rhetoric
- Students can see the pattern: anti-corruption, anti-elite, pro-"the people" messaging

**For Day 3 (Eco's 14 Points):**
- Dimitrov complements Eco by explaining the CLASS FUNCTION beneath the cultural features
- Where Eco describes what fascism LOOKS like, Dimitrov explains what it DOES

**Potential handout excerpt:**
The passage on youth susceptibility could work well - shows a historical figure in 1935 identifying the exact dynamics that still operate today.

### 11. Ernst Jünger (1920) - Storm of Steel

**Context:** WWI memoir from a highly decorated German officer. Jünger was wounded 14 times and received Germany's highest military honour. Written immediately after the war, the book became a cult text for the German right and influenced the "warrior aesthetic" that fed into National Socialism—though Jünger himself later distanced himself from the Nazis.

**Why It Matters for This Unit:** Storm of Steel represents the proto-fascist aesthetic that emerged from the trenches: the valorization of violence, the transformation of men into steel, the pursuit of intensity through destruction. This is the WWI crucible from which fascism emerged. It directly parallels Mishima's "sun and steel" imagery.

**THE FAMOUS OPENING PASSAGE - The Romanticization of War:**
> "We had come from lecture halls, school desks and factory workbenches, and over the brief weeks of training, we had bonded together into one large and enthusiastic group. Grown up in an age of security, we shared a yearning for danger, for the experience of the extraordinary. We were enraptured by war. We had set out in a rain of flowers, in a drunken atmosphere of blood and roses. Surely the war had to supply us with what we wanted; the great, the overwhelming, the hallowed experience. We thought of it as manly, as action, a merry duelling party on flowered, blood-bedewed meadows."

This passage captures everything: the boredom of bourgeois security, the yearning for intensity, the aesthetic framing of violence ("blood and roses"), the desire for transcendent experience through war.

**On the Transformation of Men into Steel:**
> "Hardened as scarcely another generation ever was in fire and flame, we could go into life as though from the anvil; into friendships, love, politics, professions, and into all destiny had in store. It is not every generation that is so favoured."

> "After [the battle of the Somme], the German soldier wore the steel helmet, and in his features, there were chiseled the lines of an energy stretched to the utmost pitch."

**On the Intensity of Combat - The "Great God Pain":**
> "This was the home of the great god Pain, and for the first time I looked through a devilish chink into the depths of his realm."

Compare to Mishima: "Pain, I came to feel, might well prove to be the sole proof of the persistence of consciousness within the flesh."

**On Trench Fighting as the Purest Combat:**
> "Trench fighting is the bloodiest, wildest, most brutal of all... Of all the war's exciting moments none is so powerful as the meeting of two stormtroop leaders between narrow trench walls. There's no mercy there, no going back, the blood speaks from a shrill cry of recognition that tears itself from one's breast like a nightmare."

**On the Dehumanizing Effect - And Its Valorization:**
> "I had a shock when I realized for the first time how callous the war had made me. One got to regarding men as mere matter."

> "Nothing was left in his voice but equanimity, apathy; fire had burned everything else out of it."

Note: Jünger doesn't present this as tragedy—he presents it as transformation into something harder, purer, more elemental.

**On Beauty Amid Violence:**
> "It is easier to go into battle amid nature's beauties than in winter's dead landscape. Somehow, it comes to one quite simply that one's existence is part of an eternal circuit, and that the death of a single individual is no great matter."

**On the Craving for Violence:**
> "The horrible was undoubtedly a part of that irresistible attraction that drew us into the war. A long period of law and order, such as our generation had behind it, produces a real craving for the abnormal, a craving that literature stimulates."

**On Patriotism as Faith Requiring Sacrifice:**
> "When once it is no longer possible to understand how a man gives his life for his country—and the time will come—then all is over with that faith also, and the idea of the Fatherland is dead; and then, perhaps, we shall be envied, as we envy the saints their inward and irresistible strength."

**On the Character of Veteran Soldiers:**
> "There was in these men a quality that both emphasized the savagery of war and transfigured it at the same time: an objective relish for danger, the chevalieresque urge to prevail in battle."

**The Disillusionment That Wasn't:**
> "The purpose with which I had gone out to fight had been used up, and no longer held."

But crucially, Jünger doesn't become anti-war. He becomes nostalgic for war. The loss of purpose isn't resolved by peace—it's a wound that festers.

**Integration with Unit:**

**For Day 4 (Mishima) - STRONG CONNECTION:**
- Jünger's "sun and steel" imagery directly anticipates Mishima's title
- Both describe the transformation of the body through pain and discipline
- Both aestheticize violence as a path to authenticity
- Both represent the intellectual articulation of warrior culture

**For Teacher Background:**
- Jünger shows where fascism's aesthetic comes from: the WWI trenches
- The generation that fought WWI and returned transformed became the foot soldiers of fascism
- The yearning for intensity, for transcendence through violence, for meaning through struggle—this is what fascism promised to restore

**Potential Addition to Unit:**
- Could add 1-2 Jünger passages alongside Mishima to show the genealogy
- Or use as bridge to WWI unit: "This is what WWI did to a generation. This is the mindset fascism would harness."

**Connection to Marinetti (already in notes via Benjamin):**
Both Marinetti and Jünger aestheticize war, but:
- Marinetti celebrates the machine, the future, speed
- Jünger celebrates the warrior, the archaic, endurance
Fascism would synthesize both: modern technology in service of ancient warrior values.

## Key Theoretical Synthesis

**The unit's central insight (implicit but could be made explicit):**

Fascism is not primarily an ideology but an aesthetic and psychological phenomenon. It offers:
- **Certainty** in the face of overwhelming complexity (Eco: cult of tradition, rejection of modernism)
- **Belonging** through enemy-definition (Eco: fear of difference, obsession with plot)
- **Meaning** through struggle and sacrifice (Eco: life is permanent warfare, cult of heroic death)
- **Beauty** through discipline, the body, and violence (Sontag, Benjamin, Mishima)

Mishima makes the aesthetic dimension visible. He pursued beauty, strength, and life at their most intense—and this pursuit led logically to death. This is fascism's core paradox: vitality worship becomes death worship.

**Why this matters pedagogically:**
Students can dismiss fascism as stupid or evil. Mishima won't let them. His brilliance, sensitivity, and self-awareness force engagement with fascism's genuine appeal. If we understand what it offered him, we understand what it offers anyone.

## THE CENTRAL QUESTION: Why Is Fascism So Seductive?

**The unit's essential question requires a satisfying answer. The sources provide one.**

### What Fascism Offers (That Liberal Democracy Does Not)

**1. INTENSITY**
Normal life under liberal capitalism is experienced as pallid, bureaucratic, meaningless. Fascism offers life at maximum intensity—every moment charged with significance because death is always proximate.

From Patriotism: The couple's final night together is the most alive they've ever been precisely because it's their last. "The distant pains of death had refined their awareness of pleasure."

From Sun and Steel: "The sun and the steel were simultaneously corroding me and building me up, strengthening my muscles while tirelessly pouring a kind of death into me."

**2. CERTAINTY**
The burden of freedom is exhausting. Choosing requires taking responsibility; being wrong requires living with the consequences. Fascism removes this burden entirely.

From the unit overview: "Fascism offers an exit from the burden of freedom. The responsibility of making choices in an uncertain world is exhausting. Fascism promises to take that burden away."

From Eco: The cult of action for action's sake. Thinking is a form of emasculation. Don't deliberate—act.

**3. BELONGING**
Atomized modern individuals lack genuine community. Fascism provides visceral belonging through shared enemies, shared rituals, shared identity.

From Sun and Steel: "Only through the group—through sharing the suffering of the group—could the body reach that height of existence that the individual alone could never attain."

From Parenti (the Schlageter dialogue): The son dismisses class conflict because "we have the Volk community." When the father asks what this means, the son replies: "It's an experience!"

**4. TRANSCENDENCE**
The self is a prison. Fascism offers dissolution of the self into something greater—nation, movement, leader, death.

From Sontag: "Fascist aesthetics endorse two seemingly opposite states, egomania and servitude." The paradox resolves: total submission enables fantasies of total power because the self is dissolved into the all-powerful collective.

From Patriotism: Reiko's existence had become her husband's existence. This is presented not as tragedy but as fulfillment.

**5. BEAUTY**
Liberal modernity is ugly—strip malls, cubicles, screens. Fascism offers aesthetic intensity: uniforms, torchlight, massed bodies, architectural grandeur, the beautiful death.

From Benjamin: "The logical result of Fascism is the introduction of aesthetics into political life."

From Marinetti (quoted by Benjamin): "War is beautiful because it initiates the dreamt-of metallization of the human body."

From Sontag: "The fascist dramaturgy centers on the orgiastic transactions between mighty forces and their puppets."

**6. MEANING THROUGH SACRIFICE**
Life under capitalism is meaningless. Fascism offers meaning through struggle, sacrifice, martyrdom. Your death matters; you die for something.

From Eco: Life is permanent warfare. The cult of heroic death. "A true hero yearns for death."

From Mishima: The body's "one fatal flaw was that muscles decline with life itself." Therefore the body must be destroyed at its peak—otherwise it just decays like everything else.

### Why This Matters Pedagogically

**Students need to feel this, not just hear it.**

The unit's encounter-first design exists because students must experience the pull before they can analyze it. If we just tell them "fascism is seductive because X, Y, Z," they'll nod and forget. If they feel it—through the Socratic circle questions, through the unlabelled contemporary voices, through Mishima's prose—they'll understand in their bodies.

**The Mishima case is essential precisely because he's undeniable.**

Students can dismiss historical fascists as ignorant, brutal, foreign. They cannot dismiss Mishima. He was brilliant, sensitive, cultured, self-aware—and he chose this. Understanding *why* he chose it is understanding why *anyone* chooses it.

**The answer isn't flattering to liberal democracy.**

The sources consistently suggest that fascism fills genuine voids in modern life. Liberal capitalism produces atomization, meaninglessness, aesthetic poverty, and the exhausting burden of freedom. Fascism exploits these real conditions.

This means anti-fascist pedagogy can't just say "fascism is bad." It has to address the conditions that make fascism appealing. Students should leave this unit asking: How do we create meaning, belonging, intensity, and beauty without the death cult?
